# On Time — History Eras Bible

> *"You do not learn history by being told. You learn it by standing in it."*
> — Kimiru (never says this aloud; it's his operating principle)

---

## How This Document Works

Each era is a **playable chapter or multi-chapter arc** within On Time.
Eras are woven into the Five Arcs — they are not side content or minigames.
They are the world the siblings travel through, and they shape who the siblings become.

The eras serve the sibling story. The sibling story never pauses for the eras.

---

## The History Era Chapter Template

Every era chapter follows this repeatable structure:

### 1. Entry Hook
- Kimiru speaks. Air changes. Sound drops. Arrival.
- The siblings are **disoriented**. They don't know where or when they are.
- First 2-3 minutes: sensory grounding. What do they see? Smell? Hear?
- One sibling notices something that anchors them (Lu reads a sign, Yen hears music,
  Rush sees tools he doesn't recognize, Drew orients by the sun).
- **No exposition.** The player figures it out through observation.

### 2. Grounded Objectives
- The siblings need something: food, shelter, safe passage, information.
- Objectives are **human-scale**: find a meal, earn trust, navigate a market,
  avoid attention, help someone, survive the night.
- The historical setting creates the constraints — not arbitrary game rules.
- Shepherd (when present) excels in eras that reward strategy and control.
  Drew excels in eras that reward compassion and patience.
  That contrast is always present.

### 3. The "Quiet Learning" Moment
- At least one scene per era where the player **learns by doing**:
  - Bargaining in a market and learning relative value
  - Watching a craft and understanding labor
  - Sitting through a ceremony and absorbing belief
  - Navigating class hierarchy and feeling its weight
  - Sharing a meal and understanding scarcity or abundance
- **No character explains the lesson.** The game trusts the player.
- Kimiru may say one short thing afterward. Or nothing.

### 4. Optional Side Conversations (Memory Flags)
- Each era has 2-4 **optional conversations** with era-specific NPCs.
- These conversations create **memory flags** that feed the Chapter 29 engine.
- Examples: a blacksmith who reminds Rush of Drew. A mother defending her child
  the way Drew defended Yen. A merchant who sounds like Shepherd.
- These are the deposits. The player doesn't know they're being stored.

### 5. Moral/Choice Tension
- Every era contains at least one **genuine moral dilemma** rooted in historical context.
- The dilemma must not have a clearly "right" answer.
- It must connect to the game's core themes: love vs. logic, sacrifice vs. survival,
  kindness vs. efficiency, holding on vs. letting go.
- The choice shifts virtue/vice balance and relationship scores.

### 6. Exit Beat
- Something in the era connects back to the sibling story.
- A line of dialogue echoes something Drew said. Or something Shepherd would say.
- Kimiru signals departure. The transition out is quieter than the arrival.
- One sibling carries something — a memory, a phrase, a feeling — into the next chapter.
- The player should feel: "That was real. Those people were real."

## The 12 Candidate Eras

### Era 1: Feudal Japan — Kyoto, ~1470 (Ōnin War Period)

**Setting:** A village on the outskirts of Kyoto during the Ōnin War. Not the battlefield.
The village. Where farmers are caught between warring factions and must choose
which army to feed — knowing the other will punish them.

**What the player learns:**
- The reality of feudal loyalty — not samurai honor films, but subsistence survival
- How ordinary people navigate power they can't control
- The concept of *giri* (obligation) vs. *ninjō* (human feeling) — a tension
  that mirrors Drew vs. Shepherd perfectly

**Human-scale scene:**
A farmer's wife hides rice from both armies. She shows Lu how to conceal
stores in the walls. Not because she's brave. Because her children are hungry
and bravery doesn't enter into it. It's Tuesday for her.

**Moral tension:**
A wounded soldier from the losing side asks for water. Helping him risks
the village. Refusing him is safe. Drew would help. Shepherd would refuse.
What does Rush do?

**Kimiru note:** This is Kimiru's home era. He is different here — more still,
more present, more pained. He knows this village. He may have known these people.
He says nothing about it. But his silence is louder than usual.

---

### Era 2: The Oregon Trail — Nebraska Territory, 1847

**Setting:** A wagon train crossing the plains. The siblings join a family
whose oxen are failing. Everything is about resource management, distance,
and the slow mathematics of survival.

**What the player learns:**
- The actual pace and danger of westward expansion (not the game, the real thing)
- Decision-making under scarcity — when to rest, when to push, what to leave behind
- The social dynamics of a wagon train: trust, gossip, hierarchy, mutual dependence

**Human-scale scene:**
A woman is burying a quilt her mother made. It's too heavy. It's slowing the wagon.
She folds it carefully, puts it in the dirt, and walks away. Yen watches.
Yen says: "But it was her mother's."
Drew: "Sometimes you have to put something down to keep going."

**Moral tension:**
Another family's child is sick. Sharing medicine means the siblings' family
might not have enough. Hoarding means a child might die.
The math is real. And it sounds like Shepherd.

---

### Era 3: Ottoman Constantinople — 1453 (Weeks Before the Fall)

**Setting:** The great city in its final weeks as a Christian capital.
Not the siege. The market. The mosque next to the church. The polyglot streets
where Greek, Turkish, Arabic, and Italian weave together.

**What the player learns:**
- How empires end — not in a day, but in the slow erosion of certainty
- Coexistence under pressure: different faiths sharing space while knowing
  that space is about to change hands
- How ordinary people prepare for catastrophe they can see coming

**Human-scale scene:**
A Greek baker and a Turkish spice merchant share tea. They've been neighbors
for twenty years. They both know what's coming. Neither mentions it.
They talk about the weather. About their children. About bread.
Rush sits with them. And understands: this is what endings look like.
Not explosions. Ordinary Tuesdays that are secretly the last.

**Moral tension:**
A family asks the siblings to smuggle them out through the walls.
Helping them means abandoning the baker, who refused to leave his shop.
"Someone has to keep the bread going. Even now."

---

### Era 4: The Mansa Musa Pilgrimage — Mali Empire, 1324

**Setting:** Traveling with a caravan during Mansa Musa's hajj.
Not the emperor's court. The caravan workers. The people who carry the gold,
water the camels, repair the road, and never get named in history books.

**What the player learns:**
- The scale and sophistication of the Mali Empire (counteracting Western-centric history)
- How wealth moves and what it costs the people who carry it
- The concept of generosity as both virtue and political tool
- Trans-Saharan trade routes, Islamic scholarship, the libraries of Timbuktu

**Human-scale scene:**
A water carrier teaches Rush how to find underground springs by watching animal tracks.
"The smart ones always know where to drink. Follow the birds at dawn."
This is knowledge that has been passed down for centuries. No book. No school. Just living.

**Moral tension:**
Mansa Musa's legendary generosity is crashing the local gold economy.
Giving is destroying. The caravan workers know it and can't say it.
Is generosity still a virtue when it causes harm? Drew would say yes.
Shepherd would say it depends on the math.

---

### Era 5: The Black Death — Florence, 1348

**Setting:** A Florentine neighborhood during the first wave of plague.
Not the grand narrative. One street. One family. The father is sick.
The mother is deciding whether to stay or flee.

**What the player learns:**
- How plague actually spread and how people understood (or didn't understand) disease
- Social breakdown under pandemic: who stays, who flees, who locks the doors
- The Flagellants, the scapegoating, the genuine faith and genuine cruelty
- How grief functions at civilizational scale

**Human-scale scene:**
Lu helps a woman mark her door. Not to warn others away.
To count the days her husband has been sick. Twelve marks.
"If he makes it to fourteen, they say he lives."
He's on twelve. She marks it carefully. Slowly.
That slowness is everything.

**Moral tension:**
The neighborhood wants to seal a house with a sick family inside.
It will save the street. It will kill the family.
Shepherd's logic, made literal, in 1348.

**Arc placement:** This era belongs in Arc IV (The Dark). Post-Drew.
Grief at civilizational scale, mirroring the siblings' personal grief.

---

### Era 6: The Underground Railroad — Maryland, 1852

**Setting:** A safe house. One night. The family hiding runaways.
Not the full journey. One night of impossible risk and impossible courage.

**What the player learns:**
- The actual mechanics of the Underground Railroad (signals, safe houses, routes)
- The legal and mortal danger faced by everyone involved
- How ordinary people chose extraordinary risk for strangers
- The weight of silence — how much depends on not speaking

**Human-scale scene:**
A woman teaches Yen to make a specific quilt pattern. It's beautiful.
It's also a map. The stitches are code. Yen doesn't realize this at first.
When she does, the quilt becomes something else entirely.

**Moral tension:**
A slave catcher is at the door. The family can turn the runaways away
(legal, safe, Shepherd-rational) or hide them (dangerous, illegal, Drew-right).
The player makes this choice knowing there will be consequences either way.

---

### Era 7: Viking Age Iceland — Althing at Thingvellir, ~1000 CE

**Setting:** The annual assembly where free men settle disputes through law,
not combat. The year Christianity was officially adopted — by democratic vote,
pragmatically, to prevent civil war.

**What the player learns:**
- Viking culture beyond the raiding myth: law, democracy, storytelling, trade
- How a society changes its entire belief system through negotiation
- The Althing as one of the world's oldest parliaments
- Saga tradition — oral history, memory as community property

**Human-scale scene:**
An old woman who is a lawspeaker (one of the few) recites a legal code
from memory. No paper. No book. Just a human mind holding a society's rules.
Rush watches her and thinks of Drew — who held the family's rules the same way.

**Moral tension:**
Two families are in blood feud. The legal resolution requires one family
to accept lesser compensation to prevent wider violence.
Justice vs. peace. Being right vs. keeping the community whole.
Drew's instinct: fairness. Shepherd's instinct: whatever ends it.

---

### Era 8: The Dust Bowl — Oklahoma, 1935

**Setting:** A farm that's dying. The family can't leave because the land
is all they have. The family can't stay because the land is killing them.

**What the player learns:**
- The ecological and economic catastrophe of the Dust Bowl
- How government policies and farming practices combined to create disaster
- The dignity and desperation of people who have no good options
- What it means to lose your home — not to war, but to dust

**Human-scale scene:**
A girl Rush's age shows him how to stuff rags around windows and doors
to keep the dust out. It doesn't really work. But the ritual of it —
the careful stuffing, the routine — is how she copes.
"If you stop trying, the house gives up too."

**Moral tension:**
The family's last cow is sick. Keeping it alive costs resources
the children need. The father says they should put it down.
The daughter says: "She's all that's left of the farm we had."
Sentiment vs. survival. Again.

---

### Era 9: Tang Dynasty Chang'an — China, ~740 CE

**Setting:** The cosmopolitan capital at the height of the Tang Dynasty.
The most populous city on Earth. Silk Road terminus. Poets, merchants,
monks, diplomats from Persia, India, Central Asia, and Korea.

**What the player learns:**
- The scale and diversity of Tang Dynasty China (counteracting monolithic Asian stereotypes)
- Silk Road trade networks — how goods, ideas, and religions traveled
- Poetry as social currency (Li Bai, Du Fu era)
- How a cosmopolitan empire manages diversity

**Human-scale scene:**
A Persian merchant teaches Lu to evaluate silk by touch — thread count, dye quality.
"Your eyes can lie. Your fingers never do." Lu's strategic mind lights up.
This is economics, taught through fingertips.

**Moral tension:**
A foreign monk is accused of heresy. The evidence is cultural misunderstanding,
not actual crime. Defending him means challenging a local magistrate.
The system isn't evil — it's just not designed for people who don't fit.

---

### Era 10: Revolutionary Haiti — Saint-Domingue, 1791

**Setting:** A plantation kitchen in the weeks before the revolt.
Not the battle. The whispered planning. The dangerous hope.
The people who have nothing to lose and everything to fear.

**What the player learns:**
- The Haitian Revolution — the only successful large-scale slave revolt in history
- The brutal economics of sugar plantations
- Vodou as resistance theology, not Hollywood stereotype
- How revolution starts: not with speeches, but with whispers in kitchens

**Human-scale scene:**
An old woman sings while she works. The song is in Creole.
It sounds like a lullaby. It is instructions. Meeting place. Time. Signal.
Drew hears it and understands before anyone else.
"It's not a song," he says quietly. "It's a plan."

**Moral tension:**
The revolt will be violent. People will die — not just oppressors.
Families will be separated. Children will be orphaned.
Is freedom worth the cost? The question has no clean answer.
It's the biggest version of the Shepherd/Drew dilemma in the game.

---

### Era 11: The Irish Famine — County Cork, 1847

**Setting:** A family deciding who eats. The potato crop has failed again.
The English landlord is exporting grain while his tenants starve.
The soup kitchen requires conversion to Protestantism to eat.

**What the player learns:**
- The mechanics of famine: not absence of food, but absence of access
- Colonial economics — how wealth extraction creates starvation
- The "souperism" controversy: trading faith for food
- Emigration as survival, not adventure

**Human-scale scene:**
A mother divides a turnip into five pieces. She gives each child a piece.
She does not take one herself. Nobody comments on this.
Rush watches. And sees Drew. Because that's what Drew always did.
Took the rug. Took the worst spot. Made it look natural.

**Moral tension:**
The soup kitchen offers food — but only if the family converts.
The mother won't. The children are starving.
A neighbor converts and is ostracized. But her children eat.
Who is right? The game does not say.

---

### Era 12: The Apollo Program — Houston, 1968

**Setting:** Not the launch. Not the moon. A living room in Houston.
A family watching the launch on television. The father works at NASA —
a janitor, not an engineer. His work matters. Nobody will remember his name.

**What the player learns:**
- Space exploration from the ground: the thousands of invisible workers
- 1968 America: assassinations, Vietnam, civil rights — the moon as escape and aspiration
- The gap between who gets remembered and who does the work
- How families hold together when one member belongs to something larger

**Human-scale scene:**
The janitor shows Rush how he cleans the control room.
Every surface. Every night. "If I miss a spot and it shorts something..."
He doesn't finish the sentence. He doesn't need to.
His care is invisible. His care is essential.

**Moral tension:**
The nation is spending billions on space while cities burn.
The janitor's wife says: "They put a man on the moon but they won't
fix the school on Martin Luther King Boulevard."
She's not anti-science. She's anti-being-forgotten.
The tension between aspiration and neglect. Between the extraordinary
and the ordinary people it leaves behind.

## Era Placement Across the Five Arcs

| Arc | Era Tone | Example Eras | Purpose |
|-----|----------|-------------|---------|
| **I — Golden Days** | Wonder, discovery, warmth | Chang'an, Mali, Iceland | The world is vast and beautiful. Drew leads. Shepherd is helpful. History is fascinating. |
| **II — The Cracks** | Unease, fracture, power shifts | Constantinople, Revolutionary Haiti | Empires ending. Trust fraying. Shepherd's philosophy gaining weight. |
| **III — The Fall** | Loss, silence | (Present day — no era travel during Drew's death) | The eras stop. The present is enough. |
| **IV — The Dark** | Grief, survival, endurance | Black Death, Dust Bowl, Irish Famine, Underground Railroad | Eras that mirror loss. People surviving what shouldn't be survivable. Breathing-room eras between heavy grief chapters. |
| **V — The Rushlight** | Rebuilding, legacy, carrying forward | Oregon Trail, Apollo Program | Moving forward. Carrying something. Building something new from what was lost. |

### Integration Notes

- **Arc I eras** should feel like field trips with a good teacher who never lectures.
  Drew is present. The kids are together. Discovery is joyful.
  Shepherd is competent and warm in these eras — he thrives in structured environments.

- **Arc II eras** shift tone. The history gets darker. The siblings see what happens
  when trust fractures. Constantinople — the last Tuesday before everything changes —
  directly mirrors Chapter 23 (The Ordinary Morning).

- **Arc III has no era travel.** Drew's death happens in the present.
  The absence of era travel after Chapter 24 is felt.
  Kimiru can't — or won't — take them anywhere.
  The present is all there is.

- **Arc IV eras** are the breathing room. When the sibling grief gets too heavy
  (per the Sustainability Rule), Kimiru takes them to an era
  where other people survived similar loss. Not to teach a lesson.
  To say: "This has happened before. They survived. So can you."
  The player gets mechanical variety (exploration, objectives)
  and emotional relief through *other people's stories*.

- **Arc V eras** are about legacy. The Oregon Trail: carrying everything you own
  into the unknown. The Apollo Program: invisible people doing essential work.
  These eras model what it looks like to build something new
  from what was lost — without pretending the loss didn't happen.

## The Anti-Lecture Rule

**In no era may a character deliver a history lesson.**

The player learns by:
- Observing the environment
- Completing objectives that require understanding the era's rules
- Talking to NPCs who speak from *their* reality, not the player's
- Making choices that only make sense if you understand the context
- Kimiru's rare, short observations after the fact

**If a scene feels like a classroom, cut it.**
**If a scene feels like a place you lived, keep it.**

The goal is not: "Now I know about the Dust Bowl."
The goal is: **"I remember that girl stuffing rags in the windows."**

Facts are forgettable. People are not.

---

## The Smugness Rule

The siblings do not arrive in the past with moral superiority.

They do not say: "This is wrong" about slavery, feudalism, or patriarchy
from a position of modern comfort. They *feel* the wrongness by experiencing
the constraints. They struggle with systems they can't change.

If the game ever feels like it's saying "look how far we've come,"
it has failed. History is not a progress narrative.
It is a collection of people doing their best inside systems
they didn't choose.

The siblings should leave every era thinking:
"Those people were as smart as us, as kind as us, and as trapped as us."

That's the real history lesson.
